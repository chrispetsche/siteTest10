A virtual experience created to provide a double means of: visually pitching redesign and update suggestions for various Lucky Strike Social locations, presenting potential offsite clients with a way of seeing the venue without actually visiting.

A project intented to assist a company I loved working for with their journey into the future!!!

Project description

After completing grad school, the goal was to create my own studio and use what I'd shared in the thesis that earned my masters degree as the foundation for doing so. While playing with a few concepts and trying to settle on my first project, since I was free of the 2 and half year crunch on my thesis, the assistant general manager at my day job knew I could model and created environments during my schooling and came to me with a request.

image above of real world venue before

The company was going through several changes at the time, and even though I was not part of management, they always welcomed my ideas and treated me as if I were one of the management team. Naturally, when you love someone or something you become passionate about making the world around that love better. The people at Lucky Strike were like an extended part of my family.

So, when the AGM came asking for ideas on how to pretty up a particularly ugly wall and try making it a focal point rather than an eye sore, I dove in head over heels.

image above of Einstein mural wall with in-engine updates

From there, redesigning a single wall quickly turned into redesigning an adjacent room. A room grew to the entire venue. Very soon, it blew up into a multi-purpose virtual tour, and is still my largest project to date.

My contributions

Pre-Development

The project was done completely solo, and went through a few stages that spand a little more than 2 years. Partly due to sometimes enjoying precision a bit more than most. But this project, needing a scale model of the whole venue and everything it contained, was all about detail. And, that made it a perfect fit for my creative drive.

image above of real world venue floor map

To make such a detailed model to scale, part of each day was spent making lists of assets, drawing a map of each asset from different angles and figuring out how to best measure them. Ranging from the large simple structural pieces, down to the salt and pepper shakers, everything in the venue was taken into account even if it may not have made it into the final build.

Then, using the drawings as a guide, each asset was meticulously measured down to an 1/8th inch of being perfectly accurate.

Lighting

Once the bulk of the assets had been taken into Unity and placed, lighting the virtual venue started adding to the excitement of the build. And to be honest was relatively simple to set up using 2 features Unity has made better and more user friendly over the years.

Basic Lighting

With the exception of a few recessed ceiling lights and hanging lamps, the majority of the lighting in the real world venue was done by using rail lights that hung from the ceiling. Each rail held several spotlights that could be angled almost any direction.

image above of old style large white rail lights
image above of in-engine updated rail light setup

Since the project was to display suggested updates, the only thing that changed was the styling. When the models were imported and assembled in-engine, a prefab was created that had a Unity spotlight attached. With the model built to have pivot points like the real world version, they too could be angled any direction.


After the light map was set up and baked in, these worked very well in combination with the ambient moon light coming in from the large windows along the east and south side of the building.

Effects Lighting

The second Unity feature I loved using to develop this project was its post processing effects. Some being baked, and others left to run in real-time, dramatically added to the look and feel of the design. Plus, they are alot of fun to play with reguardless of the project they are in!

image above of in-engine bloom effects used

The effect enjoyed the most would have to be the Bloom when applying the emission element of an assets material. Causing it to have that nice fuzzy glow effect that real world lights typically have around them, based on how bright they are and their immediate environment.


This was used on all the lights, but emphasized most at both the dining and Einstein bars, as well as around the large window that allowed guests to look out across the bowling lanes from the Einstein Room. Each explained more in the Remodel Design section of the contribtions.

image above of example in-engine ambient occlusion and vignette effects use

Other effects used in the post processing that brought an added layer of depth to the lighting, was the ambient occlusion and the vignette. Both sharped the natural looking shadows found in places that connect one asset to another, like wall seams and corners, of which the venue obviously had many.

UI/UX Design

After building out a large portion of the virtual venue, the 'touring' side of the project needed to be fulfilled. The first step to this was designing a way of interfacing with the experience in both the VR and WebGL builds. And using Unitys world-space UI canvas setup worked great for a semi-directed and semi-interactive experience such as this.

Tour Guide and Presentation Panels

image above of in-engine welcome presentation panel

A series of presentation panels containing up to 3 elements were designed. As seen in the first image of this contribution that starts off the tour, it contains a message welcoming the viewer/ guest to the location of the venue they are taking the tour of. And it wouldn't be much of tour without a guide.

The demo being done with a nicely outlined butler like silohette that would gesture at things as it spoke. For the full version an actor or even a real sales team memeber would replace the butler for the web version, and short greenscreeened videos that would definitely enhance the experience in the VR.

image above of in-engine pause point presentation panel

The message area functioned as both a caption/ subtitling system for the guide speaking to the viewer as the tour moved about the venue, as well as a display for other important information the guide might not cover. Things that would make the tour drag on forever, and may not be important to everyone. Like reading off every cocktail on the bar menu for a childs party hosted by a convent of nuns.


But, using the third element of the presentation panels allowed the viewer to interact with the tour by giving them buttons that opened menus, answered frequently asked questions and gave them nagivational choices. Like which pause point to move to.

Level Design

To complete the design of the tour, something similar to a games level design that moves players through the environment was needed. A path laid out to get the viewer from the front of the venue to the back, as well as what to display and when.

Tour Path

image above of in-engine rough tour map

Being that the sales team already had a basic path they used to show the real world version everyday, and the project would be an aid to them in the field after completion, it was just a matter of adapting one version to the other.

The path would of course start at the front doors and then wind left and right back to the private room. The guide and messaging would be the only elements displayed as the tour was in motion, with the camera being animated to follow the guide as they talked about things along the way. At specific points, as shown in the tour map, the guide would pause its movement, finish its presentation of that section and give the viewer some options to select from, as explained in the UI/UX section.


The option they had to select for the tour to continue was one of the navigation choices that consisted of a "Continue", "Back", or "Select Point" that opened a map similar to the one above that let the viewer skip around to any part of the tour they wished, with a fading out and in teleportation effect.


And with the viewer only changing from a potential client to a staff member, the same path and basic events setup could also be used to show off the remodel suggestions. Completing all the purposes of the project.

Remodel Design

Many of the designs shown in the images and videos throughout this project came from a repetitive process, that aimed to fit within the scope of having a modern industrial theme with a techy vibe to it. Something that presented itself as being up-to-date San Francisco. But also something that wasn't just aesthetic. It was also functional. One of my favorite types of design!


The design process involved creating quick versions of an in-engine build that could be shared with fellow staff members, management and non-management. Gather several thoughts, opinions and even further specific requests from management at varying levels. Then, taking all that and fuse it into another build that was again shared for the feedback.

Step and Repeat Wall

The design for the wall that started it all seemed straight forward to me. The wall ran parallel to the walls that gave the bowling section its length, and faced the same way as the backdrop that gave the lanes their visual appeal above the pins.

Extending what already existed the rest of the way down the wall connected the once very separate areas. The greyish-white around the black x pattern played in well with the white floors of the Einstein Room that had black highlights in it. And made the Control Desk, where you go to bowl or play games, stand out more. Expressing it as the obvious hub for both areas as it should have been from the beginning.

image above of wall with step and repeat designs applied in Unity

Another part of the design came from the backdrop only coming 2/3 of the way down the wall. The remaining portion could be painted a flat black like the Einstein had and would also be expanding on. This is where the functional part plays in.


Lucky Strike is known for their parties. It's also one of their largest revenue streams. From small family fun days, to massive things like hosting Super Bowl 50 where set up took over a month to complete. Small or massive, this particular wall was used for different table setups and of course taking pictures in front of the step and repeat background that was to be featured there. With that meant the lower section got hit alot, which left holes far more often than would be preferred.

With having only the bottom that needed to be painted verses the entire wall, maintaining its clean and high quality appears became dramatically easier. A function I aimed to apply throughout the entire venue.

Einstein Room

Even though the request to redesign the step and repeat wall was technically what started the project, it was being able to redesign a room that was named after one of my personal historic heros that shoved it toward what it ended up being. Every Lucky Strike venue at the time had a unique theme to it. And Albert Einstein was a part of San Francisco.

image above of Einstein Room as it was

The orignal design of the room was very plain and only had a single mural on a single wall despite the whole room being named after him. And it really didn't show the genius that his work obviously was. So I set out to display his life on all the surfaces that would hold something worth admiring for a while, including the ceiling.

Before getting too far with alot of detail, let me point out that with this room extending from the dining room, and its high ceilings, all glass store front that pointed toward the southern sun all day, and both rooms bathed in bright white, the space felt glaring from certain perspectives or washed out and hidden from others.


The idea to immediately make the room stand out from the rest was to apply a direct color contrast by expanding the flat black of the original Einstein mural to all the walls and the ceiling, as mentioned above. With the right lighting, especially at night when they dim the overhead lights and the venue holds more of a night club vibe, you can't help but gravite toward the room and want to hangout even without all the art and features shown below.

image above of Einstein Room in-engine floor map

The room was then broken down into sections. Each having a different function and its own piece of art. Some very simple, and others detailed enough that you might catch yourself staring at for a while.


Starting with the largest canvas in the room, the ceiling. Einstein did several great things. But the 2 that we really know him for, the theories of relativity, need the space to even attempt a meaningful depiction of something so beautiful. The images below show the rough concepts of what was to be a massive mural containing both on the left, and on the right placeholder images used in Unity for testing placement and scale.

image above of rough ceiling art designs
image above of ceiling art placeholders

The half of the ceiling coming in from the control desk was meant to have a visual representation of Special Relativity, expressing the fusion process occuring in stars throughout the universe. The first of the world changing theories known by the famous, E=MC^2.


On other half of the ceiling closer to the original mural wall shows General Relativity, the dualities and relationships of spacetime along with energy and matter.

image above of Young Albert wall with in-engine
design and lighting applied

To keep things from getting too overwhelming by plastering dynamic images everywhere, another concept came to mind that felt interesting if the viewer caught onto it.


On the Specialy Relativity side, a photo of young Albert with his birth and death dates.

image above of Old Eistein wall after in-engine
design and lighting applied

On the opposite side, a photo of old Einstein before he passed. And the two, old and young, looking at each other. Literally and metaphorically gazing across space and time, and his accomplishments.

The back wall, opposite the dining room, was primarily consumed by a long glass window that looked out over the lanes with a narrow wall bar beneath it. Like every other part of this room, most people never noticed the window until it was right in from of them.


Along with it being surrounded by the flat black color, the fix here was to outline the glass with a bead of lights. Tuned to the right color and level of illumination, this created an appealing transition point. Helping to highlight features of the bowling alley on other side, like the "Lucky Strike" sign at the far end. Resulting in the viewer being pulled through the venue just by giving them an interesting way of realizing there was more to it.

image above of glass wall

On either side of the window was space enough for 2 final pieces of art that finished bringing it all together. Both simple, yet still very important to the overall tribute. Looking at the wealth of knowledge Albert discovered for us with his huge personality and imagination, as well as parts of his ealier life that is never credited enough.


The first, on the left side of the window, next to the old mural wall and beneath the General Relativity. Even though the image is simple, it was meant to contain a few things.


The most obvious is something most don't know he did. Of the small handful of instruments he played, guitar was one of them. Even though he was better at piano, and more so with the violin, this image seemed to carry more of the chilled vibe. With him kicked back, strumming out a tune in his flat cap. Very much what the venue was being designed around.

The second part was a little something I wrote toward his work final and unfinished discovery that attempted to connect the biggest of the big, with the smallest of the small.

"Before his departure, combine all the forces of nature into a single simple explaination of Everything, called...
Unified Field Theory"
"This explaination would have shown many things, but above all..."
"At our most fundamental, we are all the sum of a single point in Forever, acting simultaneously as a wave, particle and field, while vibrating in every possible direction of space and time, all at once!"

With that comes that last part of the image design. If you notice, a string extending to from the guitart to the ceiling. The final rendering here would have had the string being strummed with a wave in it, of course moving out across the neck. In the section between the guitar and ceiling, its partical form would be displayed. And from there the string would be connected to the gravitational field shown with the finished General Relativity piece on the ceiling.

For the final piece of art in the room, a tribute to a very under credited individual that may have been the one to direct Einsteins brillance toward what it became. A brilliant individual herself, but held back by timing and being a woman.


Mileva Maric - Einstein was not only his first wife, but a large contributor in the discoveries that added up to Special Relativity.


So, on the right side of the window and below the mural she had part in producing, a simple image and a message of recognition for the sadly unknown role she had in helping reshape the world for individuals like her.

design and lighting applied

To top off the room, beyond the art and lighting, one more aspect needed to be fulfilled. It was referred to as a gaming lounge that consisted of a set of pool tables, a trivia machine and prize/crane machine, along with a 4 player PacMan. Personally, that barely qualified as a gaming anything.

image above of gaming map

Removing 2 very outdated machines and installing a select few modular arcade cabinets using the map shown, would have not only kept a balanced mix of game types for the diverse crowds of San Francisco, it would have justified the name of the room and its functional title. Then, putting together some next generation multi-purpose VR mods and gaming stations would have taken the space to a whole other level.

image above of multi-purpose gaming stations

In replace of the old trivia machine, that almost consumed the entire wall that had anything to do with Einstein, the general manager of the store had a request to go along with my suggestion of bringing VR into the venue.


Several TVs were being added everywhere, as we showed many sporting events and more. So he actually wanted a mega-tv to be placed there that was made up of 4 large individual screens that could be synced to show events on a single mass screen, or sell game time per screen that was linked to a Playstation or XBox in the cabinet below it.

The VR mods would work the same. By renting them in time slots. But they could also be moved around easily enough that they didn't have to stay in the one room all the time, or even in the venue. Parties could rent all the mods and have them set up in the private room for their party members to use as they wish.

image above of VR mod

And at least 1 mod would be loaded with this and other Lucky Strike tours that the sales teams would have taken to different presentation, conventions and so on to show off each location while being immersed in it.

Dining Room

The dining room was next up for a major updated. Mentioned above, it too was completely saturated in white. There was some styling to it. But it was a mix of things that didn't clash well, or fit the intended design for the venue as a whole, as seen in the images that follow.

image above east side of dining room as it was
image above west side of dining room as it was

The solution here was taking a couple things that had already been employed in the two previous areas and using the modern industrial colors to start the updating process.

From the ceiling down the greatest portion of the walls and pillars could remain white. Helping to create a clean and open feeling with the ceilling being so high, in addition to helping bounce light around the room, especially at night.


Equal to the height of the half wall in the Einstein Room, a piece of wood trim stained to the same shade as the wood on the half wall, would have been placed around all the walls and pillars to separate them into upper and lower sections. Then everything in the lower would become a passive grey.

image above west dining room with in-engine
design and lighting applied

This fixed alot of the style. But like the bottom of the step and repeat wall, these walls got hit by chairs and tables being moved around. Causing a huge amount of work to keep it all repaired and professional looking. So, many things resolved with a couple simple steps.


Next was correcting the largest feature the venue contained. The dining room bar that sat at 40 feet long by 18 feet wide, right in the middile of the room. The scale made it impressive. But that white base and open space between the halo of railroad ties above it made it feel, again, out of place and incomplete.

image above of in-engine bottom side of dining room bar
image above of in-engine dining room bar crown

The 2 images above show the update base color to be a few shades darker than the dining room walls and pillar were suggested to be. More of an middle ground between the dining room grey and the Einstein black.


Then, in combination with a string of lights running the under side of the bar as well as the top side of the halo, adding a decorative set of iron looking rings to the cables suspending above halo the bar, broke up the negative space between it and the ceiling, and crowned the feature every guest is immediately introduced to as they enter the venue in an interesting way.

The last remodeling update that went into the room was some other lighting pieces. For this, stylized barrel lights my friend and co-worker Carlos and I came up with, which were barrels cut in half that had a light element built inside.


The opening would have been covered with a piece of white tinted plexi-glass, with the red Lucky Strike letters embedded in it. Each one to be hung from the ceiling by decorative iron chains and rings, bringing the room together into one clean and complete theme.

image above of in-engine dining room barrel lights
